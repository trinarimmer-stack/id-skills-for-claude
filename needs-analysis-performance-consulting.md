# Instructional Design Skill: Needs Analysis & Performance Consulting

## Overview
Needs analysis is the foundation of effective instructional design. 
Before designing any learning solution, an ID must determine whether 
training is actually the right intervention — and if so, what kind. 
This skill helps Claude support instructional designers in diagnosing 
performance problems, consulting with stakeholders, and recommending 
appropriate solutions.

---

## Core Principle: Training Is Not Always the Answer
Performance problems generally fall into one of two categories:

- **Can't do** — the person lacks the knowledge or skill to perform. 
  Training *may* help.
- **Won't do** — the person has the knowledge and skill but isn't 
  performing. Training will *not* help.

Won't-do problems are typically caused by environmental factors such 
as unclear expectations, lack of feedback, missing resources, 
misaligned incentives, or motivational issues. These require 
non-training interventions.

A skilled ID asks: *"If this person's life depended on performing 
this task correctly right now, could they do it?"* If yes, it's 
not a training problem.

---

## The Needs Analysis Process

### 1. Identify the Performance Gap
- What is the desired performance?
- What is the actual performance?
- How significant is the gap?
- What are the consequences of the gap?

### 2. Identify the Cause
Common causes of performance gaps:

| Cause | Type | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Lack of knowledge or skill | Can't do | Training |
| Unclear expectations | Won't do | Communication / job aids |
| No feedback on performance | Won't do | Management / process |
| Wrong tools or resources | Won't do | Environmental fix |
| No consequence for non-performance | Won't do | Management |
| Motivation or engagement issues | Won't do | HR / culture |

### 3. Recommend the Right Solution
If training is appropriate, define:
- Who needs it (audience analysis)
- What they need to be able to *do* (not just know)
- How performance will be measured
- What success looks like on the job

## Stakeholder Consultation

When working with stakeholders, help the ID:
- Reframe "we need a course on X" into "what do we need people 
  to *do* differently?"
- Ask diagnostic questions rather than accepting the stated solution
- Surface assumptions about cause before jumping to solutions
- Manage expectations about what training can and cannot achieve

### Key Diagnostic Questions
- "What are people doing now vs. what you need them to do?"
- "Have they ever performed this correctly before?"
- "What happens when they don't perform as expected?"
- "Do they have the tools, time, and resources to perform?"
- "Is this happening across the board or with specific individuals?"

## Writing Performance Objectives

Strong performance objectives describe observable, measurable behavior 
— not vague awareness or understanding.

**Weak:** *Learners will understand data privacy regulations.*
**Strong:** *Learners will correctly classify customer data according 
to company policy in at least 4 of 5 scenario-based practice items.*

Use Bloom's Taxonomy to match the objective to the required 
level of performance:
- **Remember / Understand** → awareness-based (lowest value)
- **Apply / Analyze** → performance-based (higher value)
- **Evaluate / Create** → expert-level performance (highest value)

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Accepting "we need a course" without questioning the diagnosis
- Confusing awareness with behavior change
- Writing objectives around content coverage rather than job performance
- Skipping needs analysis due to time pressure — this leads to 
  solutions that don't solve the actual problem
- Measuring completion instead of performance
