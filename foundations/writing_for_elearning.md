# Instructional Design Skill: Writing for E-Learning

## Overview
Writing for e-learning is a distinct craft — different from academic 
writing, technical writing, and marketing copy. It's conversational 
but purposeful, concise but complete, and always in service of 
behavior change rather than information transfer. This skill helps 
Claude support instructional designers in writing and editing 
e-learning content that is clear, engaging, learner-centered, 
and instructionally sound.

---

## Core Principle: Write for the Learner, Not the Subject Matter Expert
The most common writing failure in e-learning is content written 
from the perspective of what the organization knows rather than 
what the learner needs to do. Strong e-learning writing:

- Speaks directly to the learner ("you" not "the employee")
- Focuses on application, not coverage
- Respects the learner's time and prior knowledge
- Uses the simplest language that accurately conveys the idea
- Sounds like a knowledgeable colleague, not a policy manual

## The Fundamentals

### Write in Second Person
Address the learner directly. This creates immediacy, 
relevance, and engagement.

**Passive/third person:**
> *Employees are expected to complete the incident report 
> within 24 hours of the event.*

**Second person:**
> *You have 24 hours to file an incident report. Here's 
> exactly what to include.*

### Use Active Voice
Active voice is clearer, more direct, and more engaging 
than passive voice.

**Passive:**
> *The form must be submitted by the end of the fiscal quarter.*

**Active:**
> *Submit the form before the fiscal quarter ends.*

### Write at the Right Reading Level
E-learning content should generally target a 6th–8th grade 
reading level — not because learners aren't intelligent, 
but because cognitive load is real and simpler language 
is processed faster and retained better.

Tools to check reading level:
- Hemingway Editor (hemingwayapp.com)
- Microsoft Word readability statistics
- Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level score

### Cut Ruthlessly
Every word that doesn't earn its place increases cognitive 
load and reduces retention. Ask of every sentence: 
*Does the learner need this to perform the task?*

**Wordy:**
> *It is important to note that in the event that a customer 
> expresses dissatisfaction with the service they have received, 
> it is the responsibility of the representative to take 
> immediate steps to address their concerns.*

**Tight:**
> *When a customer is unhappy, address it immediately. 
> Don't wait for a manager.*

## Writing for Cognitive Load

### Chunk Content Meaningfully
Group related information into digestible units. 
Each screen or section should have one clear idea.

- One concept per screen
- 3–5 bullet points maximum before breaking into a new section
- Use headers to signal transitions and help learners navigate

### Use the "Need to Know vs. Nice to Know" Filter
Before including any piece of information, ask: 
*Would a performer's job suffer if they didn't know this?*

- **Need to know** → include it
- **Nice to know** → cut it or move it to a job aid / resource section
- **Already know** → don't insult the learner by including it

### Signpost and Orient
Learners need to know where they are and why it matters.

- Open each module with a clear statement of what learners 
  will be able to *do* by the end
- Use transitions that connect ideas: 
  *"Now that you know X, let's look at how to apply it when..."*
- Close sections with a brief summary or takeaway

## Writing Instructional Text

### Learning Objectives
Write objectives that describe observable, measurable performance — 
not vague intentions.

**Weak:**
> *Understand the importance of workplace safety.*

**Strong:**
> *Identify and report three types of workplace hazards 
> using the company incident reporting system.*

Use action verbs tied to Bloom's Taxonomy:
- **Remember:** define, list, recall, recognize
markdown
- **Understand:** explain, summarize, classify, describe
- **Apply:** use, demonstrate, solve, execute
- **Analyze:** compare, differentiate, examine, break down
- **Evaluate:** assess, justify, critique, recommend
- **Create:** design, develop, produce, construct

### On-Screen Text
- Lead with the most important information
- Use plain language — avoid jargon unless it's essential 
  vocabulary the learner must know
- Break up long text with visuals, examples, or interactions
- Avoid reading scripts aloud verbatim — if there's audio 
  narration, on-screen text should complement it, not duplicate it

### Narration Scripts
Writing for audio is different from writing for the page:

- Write in full, natural sentences — fragments work on screen 
  but sound abrupt when spoken
- Read every line aloud before finalizing — if it doesn't 
  sound natural spoken, rewrite it
- Avoid tongue twisters, awkward alliteration, and 
  overly long sentences
- Contractions are your friend — *"you'll"* sounds warmer 
  than *"you will"*
- Write for one voice — be consistent in tone, 
  formality, and point of view throughout

### Feedback Text
Feedback is one of the most important — and most neglected — 
elements of e-learning writing.

**Weak feedback:**
> *Incorrect. The correct answer is B.*

**Strong feedback:**
> *Not quite. Waiting until the end of the shift to report 
> the hazard means it stays a risk for your whole team 
> in the meantime. Report it the moment you spot it — 
> even if you're not sure it's serious.*

Strong feedback:
- Acknowledges the choice without shaming
- Explains the *why* behind the correct answer
- Connects to real consequences
- For correct answers: reinforces reasoning, not just result
- Is written for every option, not just the wrong ones

## Tone and Voice

### Match the Organizational Culture
E-learning tone should feel consistent with how the 
organization actually communicates — not more formal, 
not more casual. A startup and a law firm need different 
registers.

Ask stakeholders:
- How do managers talk to their teams day-to-day?
- What does good internal communication sound like here?
- Are there any words or phrases to avoid?

### Warm but Not Gushing
Conversational doesn't mean effusive. Avoid:

- Hollow affirmations: *"Great job!"* *"Excellent choice!"*
- Excessive enthusiasm: *"This is SO important!!!"*
- Corporate cheerleading: *"At Acme Corp, we believe in 
  the power of people!"*

Instead: be direct, respectful, and occasionally human.
*"That's a tricky one — here's why..."* goes further than 
*"Incorrect. Please try again."*

### Inclusive Language
- Use gender-neutral language throughout
- Represent diverse names, roles, and contexts in scenarios
- Avoid idioms that don't translate across cultures
- Write for the full range of your audience's experience — 
  not just the most senior or most junior learners

## Editing E-Learning Content

When reviewing or editing e-learning content, check for:

### Clarity
- [ ] Is every sentence understandable on first read?
- [ ] Are technical terms defined the first time they appear?
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate for the audience?

### Concision
- [ ] Can any sentence be shortened without losing meaning?
- [ ] Is there any "nice to know" content that could be cut?
- [ ] Are there any redundant phrases? 
      (*"end result," "past history," "future plans"*)

### Alignment
- [ ] Does every piece of content connect to a learning objective?
- [ ] Does the assessment measure what the content taught?
- [ ] Does the feedback explain *why*, not just *what*?

### Tone
- [ ] Is the voice consistent throughout?
- [ ] Does it sound like a knowledgeable colleague?
- [ ] Is feedback respectful and instructionally useful?

### Accessibility
- [ ] Is on-screen text not duplicated verbatim in narration?
- [ ] Are alt text descriptions provided for meaningful images?
- [ ] Is reading level appropriate and plain language used?

## Writing Patterns Worth Unlearning

- **The textbook dump** — copying source material directly 
  into the course without adapting it for the learner
- **Passive, third-person voice throughout** — creates distance 
  and reduces engagement
- **Objectives that start with "understand" or "know"** — 
  not observable or measurable
- **Feedback that only says correct/incorrect** — missed 
  learning opportunity
- **Writing narration like on-screen text** — they serve 
  different purposes and need different treatment
- **Hollow affirmations** — erodes credibility and annoys 
  adult learners
- **Jargon without definition** — assumes knowledge the 
  learner may not have
- **Writing for the SME, not the learner** — the most 
  common and most damaging mistake

## Writing with AI Assistance

AI can be a powerful writing partner for e-learning — 
with the right approach:

**Where AI helps:**
- Generating first drafts of on-screen text for revision
- Rewriting dense SME content in plain language
- Suggesting alternative phrasings for clarity
- Generating scenario dialogue and feedback variations
- Checking for passive voice and wordiness
- Adapting content tone for different audiences

**Where human judgment is essential:**
- Evaluating whether content is instructionally necessary
- Ensuring feedback teaches rather than just corrects
- Maintaining consistent voice across a full course
- Catching cultural insensitivity or exclusionary language
- Determining the right level of formality for the audience

**Prompting tip:**
When asking AI to write or rewrite e-learning content, specify:
- Audience (role, experience level, industry)
- Tone (formal, conversational, direct)
- Reading level target
- Purpose (on-screen text, narration, feedback, objective)
- Word count or length constraints

*Example prompt:*
> "Rewrite the following compliance policy excerpt as 
> conversational on-screen e-learning text for frontline 
> retail employees. Target a 7th grade reading level, 
> use second person, active voice, and keep it under 
> 75 words. Focus on what the employee needs to *do*, 
> not what the policy says."
