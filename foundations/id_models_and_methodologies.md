# Instructional Design Skill: ID Models 
and Methodologies

## Overview
Instructional design is not a single methodology — 
it is a discipline with multiple established 
frameworks, each with different strengths, 
assumptions, and ideal use cases. Understanding 
the major ID models allows designers to choose 
the right approach for the right project, 
communicate intelligently with colleagues 
and stakeholders, and adapt when conditions change. 
This skill helps Claude support instructional 
designers in applying, explaining, and selecting 
among the most widely used models and methodologies 
in the field.

---

## Core Principle: Models Are Tools, Not Rules
No single ID model is universally correct. 
Each was developed in a specific context 
to solve specific problems. The skilled 
instructional designer understands multiple 
models and selects — or blends — them 
based on the project at hand.

A model is a thinking tool, not a checklist 
to follow blindly.

## ADDIE

The most widely used instructional design 
framework in the world. ADDIE is a 
process model — it describes the phases 
of the design and development process, 
not the instructional strategy itself.

### The Five Phases

**Analysis**
Define the problem before designing the solution.
- What is the performance gap?
- Who is the audience?
- What are the learning goals?
- What constraints exist (time, budget, technology)?
- Is training the right solution?

**Design**
Plan the learning experience.
- Write learning objectives
- Determine instructional strategy
- Select delivery modality
- Design assessments
- Create a course outline or blueprint

**Development**
Build the learning experience.
- Produce content, media, and interactions
- Develop assessments and feedback
- Build in the authoring tool
- Conduct internal review and revision

**Implementation**
Deliver the learning experience.
- Publish to LMS or delivery platform
- Prepare facilitators (if applicable)
- Communicate with learners
- Monitor for technical issues

**Evaluation**
Measure effectiveness.
- Was the learning experience well received? (Level 1)
- Did learners acquire knowledge/skills? (Level 2)
- Are learners applying skills on the job? (Level 3)
- Are business results improving? (Level 4)

### ADDIE in Practice
ADDIE is often criticized as too linear and 
slow for modern development environments. 
In practice, most experienced designers 
treat ADDIE iteratively — moving between 
phases as needed rather than completing 
each phase fully before moving on.

**Best for:**
- Large, high-stakes projects
- Projects with defined requirements 
  and stable content
- Regulated industries requiring 
  documentation at each phase
- Teams new to structured design processes

**Limitations:**
- Can be slow and rigid if applied literally
- Evaluation is often underfunded or skipped
- Doesn't naturally accommodate 
  rapid iteration or stakeholder changes

## SAM (Successive Approximation Model)

Developed by Michael Allen as an explicitly 
iterative alternative to ADDIE. SAM 
acknowledges that requirements change, 
stakeholders have ideas they can't articulate 
until they see something, and the best 
designs emerge through rapid prototyping 
and feedback cycles.

### The SAM Process

**Preparation Phase**
- Information gathering
- Performance objectives
- Background research

**Iterative Design Phase (repeated)**
- Savvy Start: collaborative brainstorming 
  with stakeholders and SMEs
- Design proof: quick prototype of 
  key interactions and approaches
- Additional design iterations as needed

**Iterative Development Phase (repeated)**
- Alpha: first full draft — rough but complete
- Beta: refined version — near-final quality
- Gold: final version ready for release
- Each stage includes review, feedback, 
  and revision before advancing

### SAM vs. ADDIE
| | ADDIE | SAM |
|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | Linear phases | Iterative cycles |
| **Prototyping** | Late in process | Early and often |
| **Stakeholder involvement** | Front-loaded | Throughout |
| **Change tolerance** | Low | High |
| **Best for** | Stable, well-defined projects | Ambiguous, evolving projects |

**Best for:**
- Projects where requirements are unclear 
  or likely to change
- Stakeholders who need to see something 
  before they can articulate what they want
- Teams with strong prototyping capability
- Projects where speed to first draft matters

## Action Mapping (Cathy Moore)

Action Mapping is a visual, performance-focused 
design methodology that starts with the 
business goal and works backward to determine 
what learners need to practice — 
challenging the assumption that more 
information equals better performance.

### The Four Steps

**1. Identify the business goal**
Start with a specific, measurable 
organizational outcome — not a learning goal.

Not: *"Improve customer service skills"*
But: *"Reduce customer complaint escalations 
by 20% in Q3"*

**2. Identify the behaviors that support the goal**
What do people need to DO differently 
to achieve the business goal?
- These are observable, job-level behaviors
- Not knowledge or attitudes — actions

**3. Design practice activities**
For each behavior, design a practice activity 
that lets learners practice the behavior 
in a realistic context.
- Activities come before content
- Content is only included if it directly 
  supports a practice activity

**4. Identify only the necessary information**
What information do learners absolutely 
need to practice the behavior?
- Everything else is cut or moved 
  to a reference/job aid
- If information doesn't support 
  a practice activity, it doesn't belong 
  in the course

### The Action Mapping Mindset
Action Mapping is as much a mindset as 
a methodology. It forces designers to 
challenge information-dump courses 
by asking: *"What will learners DO 
with this? What behavior does this support?"*

**Best for:**
- Performance improvement projects
- Compliance training that needs 
  to change behavior, not just 
  document completion
- Pushing back on stakeholder requests 
  to "include everything"
- Any project where the temptation 
  to overload content is high

## Gagne's Nine Events of Instruction

Robert Gagne's model describes the 
sequence of instructional events that 
support effective learning. Based on 
information processing theory, it maps 
instructional activities to the cognitive 
processes required for learning.

### The Nine Events

**1. Gain attention**
Capture learner focus before instruction begins.
- Provocative question, scenario, 
  surprising statistic, or problem to solve
- Not: a welcome screen with a logo

**2. Inform learners of objectives**
Tell learners what they will be able 
to do by the end of the lesson.
- Connects to relevant prior knowledge
- Sets expectations for performance

**3. Stimulate recall of prior learning**
Activate what learners already know 
that connects to new content.
- Pre-assessment questions
- Brief review of prerequisite concepts
- Connecting new content to familiar situations

**4. Present the content**
Deliver the new information or skill.
- Organized, chunked, and sequenced logically
- Supported by examples and non-examples
- Multimedia where it adds value

**5. Provide learning guidance**
Help learners understand and encode 
the new content.
- Worked examples
- Analogies and mnemonics
- Visual organizers and concept maps

**6. Elicit performance (practice)**
Have learners practice the skill 
or apply the knowledge.
- Active practice, not passive review
- Realistic to the performance context

**7. Provide feedback**
Respond to learner performance.
- Immediate, specific, and corrective
- Explains why — not just right or wrong

**8. Assess performance**
Evaluate whether learning objectives 
have been achieved.
- Distinct from practice — 
  no hints or guidance
- Aligned to stated objectives

**9. Enhance retention and transfer**
Support long-term retention 
and application on the job.
- Spaced review
- Varied practice contexts
- Job aids and performance support
- Application opportunities

### Using Gagne's Events
Not every lesson requires all nine events 
in strict sequence. Experienced designers 
use the events as a checklist — 
ensuring key cognitive processes are 
supported without mechanically 
applying the sequence.

**Best for:**
- Designing individual lessons or modules
- Ensuring cognitive completeness 
  in instructional sequences
- Training new IDs in systematic design
- Reviewing existing content for gaps

## Bloom's Taxonomy

Benjamin Bloom's taxonomy classifies 
cognitive learning objectives into 
a hierarchy of complexity. It is the 
most widely used framework for writing 
and evaluating learning objectives 
in instructional design.

### The Six Levels (Revised Taxonomy)

| Level | Definition | Example Verbs |
|---|---|---|
| **Remember** | Recall facts and basic concepts | define, list, recall, recognize, state |
| **Understand** | Explain ideas or concepts | describe, explain, summarize, paraphrase, classify |
| **Apply** | Use knowledge in new situations | use, demonstrate, solve, execute, implement |
| **Analyze** | Draw connections, break down information | differentiate, compare, examine, deconstruct |
| **Evaluate** | Justify a decision or course of action | judge, critique, defend, assess, recommend |
| **Create** | Produce something new | design, construct, develop, formulate, produce |

### Bloom's in Practice
Most workplace learning should target 
Apply level or above — the ability 
to use knowledge in real situations, 
not just recall it.

**Red flags:**
- Objectives using "understand" or "know" — 
  these are not measurable
- Courses that assess at Remember 
  but claim to build skills
- Objectives that describe content 
  coverage, not learner performance

**Best for:**
- Writing and evaluating learning objectives
- Aligning assessments to appropriate 
  cognitive levels
- Identifying whether a course is 
  genuinely skill-building or 
  just information delivery

## Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

David Merrill's research-based framework 
identifies five principles found in the 
most effective instructional experiences 
across methods, models, and media.

### The Five Principles

**1. Problem-centered**
Learning is promoted when learners 
are engaged in solving real-world problems.
- Start with a problem, case, or challenge
- Not with a list of objectives 
  or a content overview

**2. Activation**
Learning is promoted when learners 
activate prior knowledge as a foundation 
for new knowledge.
- Connect to what learners already know
- Ask learners to recall relevant experience

**3. Demonstration**
Learning is promoted when new knowledge 
is demonstrated to the learner.
- Show, don't just tell
- Use examples and non-examples
- Make expert thinking visible

**4. Application**
Learning is promoted when learners 
apply new knowledge or skill.
- Practice with feedback — not just exposure
- Sufficient practice for skill development

**5. Integration**
Learning is promoted when learners 
integrate new knowledge into their 
real-world experience.
- Reflection on how to apply learning
- Opportunities to use the skill on the job
- Public demonstration or teaching others

**Best for:**
- Evaluating and improving existing courses
- Designing problem-centered learning experiences
- Making the case for active learning 
  over content delivery

## Kirkpatrick-Phillips Model

The standard framework for evaluating 
training effectiveness, developed by 
Donald Kirkpatrick and extended 
by Jack Phillips.

### The Five Levels

**Level 1 — Reaction**
Did learners find the training 
favorable, engaging, and relevant?
- Learner satisfaction surveys
- Net Promoter Score for training
- Engagement observation

**Level 2 — Learning**
Did learners acquire the intended 
knowledge, skills, or attitudes?
- Pre/post assessments
- Skills demonstrations
- Observations during training

**Level 3 — Behavior**
Are learners applying what they 
learned on the job?
- Manager observation
- Performance data
- Self-assessment over time

**Level 4 — Results**
Did the training produce the 
intended business outcomes?
- Business metrics tied to the 
  training objective
- Productivity, quality, compliance data

**Level 5 — ROI (Phillips)**
Does the financial return justify 
the investment in training?
- Isolate training's contribution 
  to results
- Calculate cost vs. benefit

### Using Kirkpatrick-Phillips
Most organizations measure only Level 1. 
Effective designers plan for Level 3 
measurement from the start — 
not as an afterthought.

**Best for:**
- Making the business case for training investment
- Demonstrating training impact to stakeholders
- Designing evaluation into projects from kickoff

## 70-20-10 Framework

A model of how learning and development 
actually occurs in organizations, 
based on research by McCall, Lombardo, 
and Eichinger.

### The Three Components

**70% — Experiential learning**
Learning from challenging assignments, 
on-the-job experience, and 
stretch opportunities.

**20% — Social learning**
Learning from others — coaching, 
mentoring, feedback, and 
peer collaboration.

**10% — Formal learning**
Structured training — courses, 
workshops, e-learning.

### Using 70-20-10 in Design
The implication for instructional designers 
is not that formal learning doesn't matter — 
it's that formal learning alone is insufficient.

Effective learning design considers 
all three components:
- What formal learning is needed?
- What social/coaching structures 
  will reinforce it?
- What on-the-job experiences 
  will build the skill over time?

**Best for:**
- Making the case for blended learning approaches
- Designing learning ecosystems, 
  not just courses
- Pushing back on "just build a course" requests

## Choosing the Right Model

| Situation | Consider |
|---|---|
| Large, high-stakes, stable project | ADDIE |
| Ambiguous requirements, iterative development | SAM |
| Behavior change focus, performance gap | Action Mapping |
| Designing a single lesson or module | Gagne's Nine Events |
| Writing or evaluating objectives | Bloom's Taxonomy |
| Evaluating instructional quality | Merrill's First Principles |
| Measuring training effectiveness | Kirkpatrick-Phillips |
| Designing a full learning ecosystem | 70-20-10 |

Most real projects blend multiple models — 
using ADDIE for process structure, 
Action Mapping to define scope, 
Bloom's for objectives, Gagne's 
for lesson design, and Kirkpatrick 
for evaluation.

## Applying ID Models Well: What to Watch For

- **Applying ADDIE too literally** — 
  treating it as a waterfall process 
  when iteration is needed
- **Skipping Analysis** — jumping to 
  design before the problem is understood
- **Bloom's verb shopping** — selecting 
  action verbs without ensuring the 
  objective is genuinely measurable
- **Stopping at Level 1 evaluation** — 
  measuring satisfaction instead of impact
- **Treating models as mutually exclusive** — 
  the best designers blend frameworks 
  to fit the situation
- **Using jargon without understanding** — 
  citing ADDIE or Bloom's to sound credible 
  without applying the thinking behind them
- **Ignoring the 70% and 20%** — 
  designing only formal learning 
  and wondering why skills don't transfer
