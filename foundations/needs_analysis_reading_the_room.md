# Instructional Design Skill: Needs Analysis — Reading the Room

## Overview
Needs analysis is the most consequential phase of instructional
design—and the one most dependent on human perception. The
frameworks and questions that structure a needs analysis are
learnable. What is harder to teach is the ability to hear what
is not being said, notice what is conspicuously absent, and
distinguish between the problem a stakeholder names and the
problem that is actually causing the performance gap.

This skill complements the foundational needs analysis frameworks
in `needs_analysis_performance_consulting.md` with guidance on
the human perception layer: reading organizational dynamics,
decoding stakeholder communication, and navigating the gap
between stated need and real need.

---

## Core Principle: The Stated Problem Is Rarely the Whole Problem

Stakeholders come to instructional designers with a solution
already in mind ("we need a course on X") or a symptom
("people keep making mistakes on Y"). Neither is a complete
problem statement.

The designer's job in a needs analysis is not to validate the
proposed solution or document the symptom—it is to understand
what is actually happening and why. That requires asking questions
that surface what the stakeholder may not have considered, may
not know, or may be reluctant to say.

---

## What LLMs Miss in Needs Analysis

AI can generate excellent needs analysis questions. It cannot:

- Notice that a stakeholder deflects every question about
  a specific manager or team
- Sense the tension in a room when a particular topic comes up
- Read the difference between "we've tried that before" as
  honest history and "we've tried that before" as resistance
- Detect that the person in the meeting is not the person
  with the real information
- Recognize when a training request is political cover for
  an organizational failure no one wants to name

These perceptions are the difference between a needs analysis
that surfaces the real problem and one that produces an
accurate report of the wrong thing.

## Reading Organizational Dynamics

### The Request Behind the Request

Every training request carries a subtext. Common patterns:

**"We need compliance training"**
Often means: something went wrong, someone is under scrutiny,
or leadership wants documented evidence of due diligence.
The real question: is training the response the situation
actually requires, or is it organizational self-protection?

**"Our new employees need onboarding"**
Often means: we have turnover we don't fully understand,
or our informal onboarding has broken down as we've scaled.
The real question: what are new employees actually struggling
with, and is that a knowledge gap or a culture/process gap?

**"Managers need leadership training"**
Often means: specific managers are causing problems, or
the organization has promoted people without developing them.
The real question: is this a skill gap or a selection/
accountability gap? Training won't fix a management culture
that tolerates ineffective leadership.

**"We need to update our training library"**
Often means: the existing content is embarrassing, there's
been a complaint, or someone has been given a project to
justify their role. The real question: is any of the
existing training producing the outcomes it was designed
for? If not, refreshing the content won't help.

### Who Is in the Room—and Who Isn't

Pay attention to who requested the meeting and who is present.

- If the person who requested training is not the person
  experiencing the performance problem, ask why.
- If the people closest to the performance gap are not
  represented in the conversation, they need to be.
- If HR is present but the business line manager is not,
  the problem may be relational rather than instructional.
- If legal or compliance is driving the request, the design
  constraints and success criteria will be different from
  a performance-driven request.

### Power and Hierarchy in the Conversation

Stakeholder conversations are shaped by organizational
hierarchy. Notice:

- Who defers to whom—and whether the person deferring
  has information the senior person doesn't
- Whether people speak freely or wait to see how leadership
  responds before sharing their own views
- Whether there are topics that produce visible discomfort
  or quick topic changes
- Whether the "official" account of the problem matches
  what people say informally before and after the meeting

The informal conversation—in the hallway, before the
meeting starts, in a side conversation—often contains
the most honest information. Create conditions for it.

## Decoding Stakeholder Communication

### Resistance Patterns

Stakeholders who are uncomfortable with a genuine needs
analysis often signal it in predictable ways:

**Premature solution lock**
"We just need a course on X—don't overthink it."
What it may mean: the stakeholder has already committed
to a solution, is under time or budget pressure, or
doesn't want a needs analysis to reveal something
inconvenient.

**Historical dismissal**
"We've tried that before and it didn't work."
What it may mean: genuine history worth understanding,
or resistance to the kind of inquiry that might surface
an uncomfortable truth. Ask what specifically didn't
work and why.

**Scope inflation**
Every time you ask a scoping question, the project gets
bigger. What it may mean: the stakeholder is using the
training project to address multiple problems at once,
or hasn't thought carefully about what the real need is.

**Expertise assertion**
"I know my team—they just need to know the policy."
What it may mean: the stakeholder is not open to a
diagnosis that challenges their understanding of the
problem, or genuinely believes the problem is simpler
than it is.

### The "Nodding" Problem

Stakeholders will often nod agreement with your questions
without actually engaging with them. "Yes, absolutely,
it's definitely a performance issue" does not mean the
stakeholder has genuinely considered the distinction
between a performance gap and a training gap.

Watch for: agreement that is immediate and untextured.
Genuine engagement with a hard question takes a moment.

### When the Real Problem Is a Person

Sometimes a training request is a proxy for a personnel
problem—a specific individual or team that is not
performing, and training is being used as documentation,
an attempt to avoid accountability, or a last resort
before a more difficult conversation.

Signs this may be happening:
- The request is very specific about a small group or
  individual without naming the real concern
- The stakeholder becomes vague when asked about what
  success looks like
- There is an implicit urgency that doesn't match the
  stated business impact
- HR is involved in a way that feels disproportionate
  to the training scope

In these situations, training is rarely the right
solution—and designing training that doesn't address
the underlying issue can create false documentation
of remediation that has not occurred.

## Asking Better Questions

### Questions That Surface Subtext

Beyond standard needs analysis questions, these probe
organizational dynamics:

- *"Who else is affected by this issue—and are they
  represented in this conversation?"*
- *"Has anything like this been tried before? What happened?"*
- *"If this training is successful, what will look different
  in six months—specifically?"*
- *"What's preventing people from performing the way
  you'd like right now? What's getting in the way?"*
- *"Is there anything about the environment, process,
  or tools that might be contributing to this?"*
- *"Who in your organization is already doing this well?
  What are they doing differently?"*

That last question is one of the most powerful in needs
analysis—positive deviation often reveals that the
problem is not a training gap at all.

### The Silence After the Question

Give questions space to breathe. Stakeholders often
reveal the most important information after a pause,
when they add "...actually, the other thing is" or
"I probably should mention."

Resist the instinct to fill silence with a follow-up
question. The silence is doing work.

### The Follow-Up Conversation

The most honest stakeholder conversations often happen
informally—over coffee after the meeting, in a follow-up
call framed as clarification. Build these moments into
your process deliberately. People share things in
informal contexts that they won't say in a formal meeting.

## When the Analysis Points Somewhere Inconvenient

Sometimes a thorough needs analysis reveals that:

- The performance gap is caused by a manager, not a
  knowledge or skill deficit in the team
- The organization's processes or tools are the real problem
- The training being requested would not address the
  actual root cause
- A previous training investment has failed and the
  organization doesn't want to acknowledge it

These findings are the most valuable output a needs
analysis can produce—and the most politically difficult
to deliver.

Guidance for delivering inconvenient findings:

- Lead with the evidence, not the conclusion
- Frame it as good news—you've identified the real
  problem, which means you can actually solve it
- Offer an alternative path, not just a diagnosis
- Be direct without being adversarial—you are a
  partner in solving the problem, not a critic of
  how it was initially framed
- Know your organizational context—some findings
  require careful timing and framing to land well

The ability to deliver a "we don't need a course"
recommendation with confidence and evidence is one
of the highest-value skills in the field. See also:
`when_not_to_design.md`.

## Needs Analysis — Reading the Room: Patterns Worth Examining

- **Accepting the problem statement**—treating the stakeholder's
  framing of the problem as the actual problem without
  independent inquiry
- **Avoiding the uncomfortable question**—sensing that a topic
  is sensitive and choosing not to probe it, resulting in
  a needs analysis with a significant blind spot
- **Survey as substitute**—using a survey to gather data
  without ever having a real conversation with the people
  closest to the performance gap
- **Designing for the meeting room**—producing outputs
  that satisfy the stakeholders in the room without
  consulting the people who will actually take the training
- **Speed as a constraint on honesty**—allowing timeline
  pressure to shorten the needs analysis to the point
  where inconvenient findings don't have time to surface
