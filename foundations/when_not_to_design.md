# Instructional Design Skill: When Not to Design

## Overview
One of the highest-value skills in instructional design is also
the least celebrated: knowing when training is the wrong answer—
and having the professional confidence to say so.

LLMs are predisposed to produce something. Given a training
request, an AI will generate an outline, a set of objectives,
a scenario structure. It is not predisposed to write a memo
explaining why the organization should not build a course.

This skill helps Claude support instructional designers in
recognizing non-training solutions, building the case for
restraint, and communicating findings that organizations
sometimes don't want to hear.

---

## Core Principle: Training Can Only Fix a Training Problem

Training is the right solution when—and only when—a performance
gap exists because people lack the knowledge, skill, or practice
they need to perform differently. Training cannot fix:

- A broken process
- A tool that doesn't work
- Unclear expectations or conflicting priorities
- A management or accountability failure
- An incentive structure that rewards the wrong behavior
- A resource, staffing, or workload problem
- A selection problem (the wrong people in the role)
- An organizational culture that works against the desired behavior

When training is applied to any of these problems, it produces
one of two outcomes: it fails visibly, or it appears to succeed
while the real problem continues.

---

## The Is-It-Really-A-Training-Problem Test

Before designing anything, apply these diagnostic questions:

**1. Could people perform correctly if their lives depended on it?**
If the answer is yes, the problem is not a training problem.
People who know how to do something but don't do it are facing
a motivation, incentive, process, or environment problem—
not a knowledge gap.

**2. Have they ever performed this correctly before?**
If yes: what changed? If the change is environmental, process-
based, or managerial, training will not restore the performance.

**3. What happens when they don't perform correctly?**
If the consequences of poor performance are invisible, delayed,
or absorbed by someone else, the problem is an accountability
or feedback loop issue—not a knowledge issue.

**4. Is anyone in the organization currently performing this well?**
If yes: find out what they know or do differently. If the answer
is "nothing—they just have a better manager/more resources/
clearer expectations," the solution is not training.

**5. What would change if everyone completed this training?**
If the honest answer is "not much," the training is not
addressing the root cause.

## The Non-Training Solutions Map

When training is not the answer, here are the actual solutions:

| Root cause | Appropriate solution |
|---|---|
| People don't know what's expected | Clear performance standards, documented expectations |
| Process is broken or unclear | Process redesign, job aids, decision trees |
| Tools don't support the desired behavior | Tool redesign or replacement, UX improvement |
| No feedback on performance | Performance management systems, manager coaching |
| Wrong incentive structure | Compensation or recognition redesign |
| Understaffing or resource constraint | Resource allocation, workload review |
| Management failure | Manager coaching, accountability structures |
| Selection problem | Hiring criteria, role redesign |
| Culture works against desired behavior | Culture change initiative, leadership modeling |
| One-time knowledge need | Job aid, reference tool, performance support |

## Job Aids vs. Courses

One of the most common unnecessary course designs is a course
built to solve a problem that a well-designed job aid would
solve better.

**A course is the right tool when:**
- Learners need to develop judgment or skill through practice
- The knowledge is complex and requires contextualized application
- Learners need to internalize information (not just reference it)
- The skill requires deliberate practice with feedback

**A job aid is the right tool when:**
- The task is performed infrequently enough that memorization
  is not realistic or necessary
- The task is procedural and can be broken into clear steps
- The consequences of error are high enough that on-the-job
  reference is safer than recall
- The information changes frequently enough that a course
  would quickly become outdated

The fastest, most effective solution to many training requests
is a one-page reference guide, a decision tree, or a checklist—
not a 30-minute course.

## Performance Support as an Alternative

Performance support meets people where they are, in the moment
of need, with exactly what they need to perform. Unlike training,
which builds capability in advance, performance support provides
capability at the point of application.

Performance support is often the better solution when:
- The task is complex but infrequent
- The stakes of error are high
- The learner population is experienced and doesn't need
  foundational knowledge—just reliable reference at key moments
- Speed to deployment matters

Forms of performance support:
- Decision trees and flowcharts
- Checklists and protocols
- Searchable knowledge bases
- In-app guidance and tooltips
- Quick reference cards
- Annotated templates
- Expert chat or helpdesk access

## Writing the "We Don't Need a Course" Recommendation

Delivering a finding that contradicts the original request
requires care—but also directness. A vague recommendation
that "training alone may not be sufficient" does not help
the organization solve its problem.

### Structure of a Strong Non-Training Recommendation

**1. Acknowledge the original concern**
Confirm that you have understood the business problem the
stakeholder is trying to solve. Do not make them feel
that their concern was wrong—only that the proposed
solution won't address it.

**2. Present the evidence**
Share what the needs analysis revealed—specifically what
the root cause analysis shows about why the performance
gap exists. Lead with findings, not conclusions.

**3. Explain why training won't solve it**
Be direct. "A training course would not address this
because..." is more useful than hedged language that
leaves room for the stakeholder to hear what they
want to hear.

**4. Propose the actual solution**
Never deliver a "no" without a "here's what would work."
Name the root cause and the appropriate intervention.
If the solution is outside the ID team's scope (a
process redesign, a management accountability issue),
name who should own it.

**5. Offer what training CAN do**
In many cases, the best recommendation is not "no
training" but "a smaller, different training plus
these other interventions." Training may play a
supporting role even when it is not the primary solution.

### Sample Language

*"Based on our analysis, the performance gap in X is not
primarily a knowledge issue—the team understands the
policy and could describe the correct process if asked.
The gap is driven by [specific root cause]. A training
course would not change this because [specific reason].
What we recommend instead is [specific intervention].
We can support this with [any training component that
is genuinely useful], but building a full course on
this topic would not produce the outcome you're looking for."*

## Organizational Dynamics of Saying No

### Why Training Gets Requested Anyway

Even when training is not the right solution, organizations
request it for predictable reasons:

- **Plausible action:** Training feels like doing something
  in response to a problem, even when it doesn't address
  the cause
- **Documentation:** Training creates a record that the
  organization responded to an issue—useful for legal,
  compliance, or regulatory purposes regardless of impact
- **Avoidance:** Training avoids the harder conversation
  about management failure, process dysfunction, or
  organizational accountability
- **Budget and headcount:** Training budgets are often
  easier to access than process redesign or technology
  investment budgets

Understanding why training was requested helps you frame
your recommendation in terms the organization can receive.

### When Training Is Required Regardless

Sometimes training is required by regulation, policy, or
contract—regardless of whether it will produce behavior
change. In these cases:

- Be honest with yourself about what the training can
  and cannot accomplish
- Design for the regulatory requirement without pretending
  it is a performance solution
- Recommend complementary interventions that address
  the actual performance gap if one exists
- Do not gold-plate required compliance training with
  elaborate design that won't change outcomes—apply
  appropriate effort for the actual purpose

## When Not to Design: Patterns Worth Examining

- **Solution before diagnosis**—accepting a training request
  and beginning design before completing a genuine needs
  analysis
- **The course reflex**—defaulting to a course when a job
  aid, performance support, or process change would solve
  the problem faster and better
- **Effort as evidence of value**—building elaborate training
  to justify the ID team's existence or budget, rather than
  recommending the intervention that actually solves the problem
- **Partial truth**—delivering a recommendation that training
  "alone may not be sufficient" without naming what is actually
  needed, leaving the stakeholder to draw incorrect conclusions
- **Designing for the request, not the problem**—treating
  the stakeholder's solution as the problem statement and
  designing something that satisfies the request without
  solving the real issue
