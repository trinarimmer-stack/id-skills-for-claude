# Instructional Design Skill: Culturally Situated Scenario Writing

## Overview
A scenario set in a Lagos startup feels different from one set
in a Seoul chaebol. A customer service scenario in Brazil carries
different interpersonal norms than the same scenario in Germany.
A compliance scenario in a US healthcare system operates under
different cultural pressures than one in the UK's NHS.

These differences are not surface details. They are the texture
of lived experience—and getting them wrong produces scenarios
that feel generic at best, and actively alienating or
misleading at worst.

This skill helps Claude support instructional designers in
writing scenarios that are genuinely situated in specific
cultural contexts, rather than scenarios that apply
surface diversity markers to a culturally unmarked default.

---

## Core Principle: Specificity Creates Credibility

Generic scenarios—scenarios that could take place anywhere,
with anyone, in any organization—feel thin. Learners recognize
when a scenario doesn't reflect how their world actually works,
and that recognition breaks the emotional investment that makes
scenario-based learning effective.

Cultural specificity is not about getting every detail right.
It is about grounding the scenario in enough real texture that
learners feel the designer understood their context.

---

## What LLMs Default To

Without explicit cultural guidance, AI-generated scenarios
default to a specific unmarked context:

- English as the primary and unquestioned working language
- US or Northern European workplace norms (direct communication,
  individual accountability, flat hierarchy signals)
- Western professional dress and physical settings
- Names and characters that skew Anglo-American even when
  ostensibly diverse
- Interpersonal dynamics that assume a particular relationship
  to authority, conflict, and professional communication
- Legal and regulatory references that reflect US or UK context

This default is not neutral. It centers one cultural context
as universal and treats all others as variations requiring
special handling.

## Dimensions of Cultural Difference That Matter in Scenarios

### Communication Style

**High-context vs. low-context communication**
In high-context cultures (Japan, Korea, China, many Middle
Eastern and Latin American contexts), meaning is conveyed
through relationship, subtext, and what is not said as much
as through explicit statement. In low-context cultures
(Germany, Netherlands, US, Australia), direct, explicit
communication is the professional norm.

A scenario about delivering difficult feedback will feel
and play out very differently in these contexts. The "correct"
choice in a high-context communication culture may involve
indirection that would read as evasive in a low-context culture.

**Hierarchy and deference**
How much deference is expected toward senior colleagues,
managers, or clients varies significantly across cultures—
and determines what "speaking up" or "raising a concern"
actually looks and costs in a given scenario.

**Conflict and disagreement**
Some professional cultures treat direct disagreement as a
sign of engagement and respect. Others treat it as a face-
threatening act requiring careful navigation. Scenarios about
ethical pushback, performance conversations, or stakeholder
management must reflect the specific cultural norms around
conflict in the scenario's context.

### Organizational Structure and Power

- The relationship between individual initiative and
  collective decision-making
- How formal authority is signaled and respected
- Whether hierarchy is rigid or permeable
- The role of relationship and trust-building before
  task-focused work can begin

### Professional Norms

- Meeting culture: agenda-driven vs. relationship-building;
  punctuality norms; who speaks and when
- Email and communication formality
- Work/life integration expectations
- Physical workspace norms and personal space
- Dress codes and what they signal
- The role of socializing in professional relationship-building

### Regulatory and Legal Context

Compliance scenarios must reflect the specific regulatory
environment of the scenario's setting. GDPR is not HIPAA.
UK employment law is not US employment law. Designing a
workplace harassment scenario without knowing the legal
context of the scenario's setting produces guidance that
may be incorrect or misleading.

## Designing for Specific Cultural Contexts

### Step 1: Name the Context Explicitly

Before writing, establish:
- Country or region
- Industry (industries have their own cultures that
  intersect with national culture)
- Organization type (multinational, local enterprise,
  government, startup, NGO)
- Team composition (monocultural, multicultural, remote,
  distributed)
- Character backgrounds and relevant cultural context

The more specific the context, the more useful the scenario.

### Step 2: Identify the Cultural Tension Points

Every scenario has decision points. For each decision point, ask:

- What cultural norms are relevant to this choice?
- What would a reasonable, well-intentioned person from
  this cultural context do—and why?
- What would a reasonable person from a different cultural
  context do differently—and why?
- Is there a genuine cultural difference here, or am I
  projecting a difference that doesn't exist?

### Step 3: Write From Inside the Context

The scenario should feel like it was written by someone
who knows this context—not by someone applying cultural
facts from the outside.

Markers of writing from the inside:
- Dialogue that sounds like how people in this context
  actually speak to each other professionally
- Situations that reflect the specific pressures,
  constraints, and dynamics of this environment
- Decision points that carry the real social and
  professional costs of this context
- Resolution paths that are achievable within the
  norms of this culture

### Step 4: Get Cultural Review

For scenarios set in cultural contexts the designer
does not personally inhabit, expert review is not
optional—it is a quality requirement.

Who to involve:
- Colleagues or consultants with professional experience
  in the specific cultural context
- Members of the learner population who can evaluate
  authenticity and resonance
- Subject matter experts who can verify regulatory,
  legal, or professional accuracy

What to ask reviewers to evaluate:
- Does this feel like somewhere real to someone who
  has worked there?
- Are there moments where the dialogue, choices, or
  dynamics feel wrong or foreign?
- What's missing that would make this feel more authentic?
- Are there stereotypes or errors that need correction?

## Multicultural and Cross-Cultural Scenarios

Some scenarios are designed specifically for multicultural
teams or cross-cultural encounters—situations where cultural
difference is the learning content itself.

Guidance for these scenarios:

**Avoid the cultural collision framing**
Scenarios where cultural difference is presented as a
problem to be solved or managed often inadvertently frame
non-Western norms as the source of difficulty. Design
for mutual learning and navigation, not cultural correction.

**Show genuine ambiguity**
Cross-cultural misunderstandings rarely have a clear
villain or a clear right answer. The most effective
cross-cultural scenarios show reasonable people,
operating within their own norms, producing friction—
and then navigating it with curiosity rather than judgment.

**Reflect the multicultural team's own norms**
Long-standing multicultural teams develop their own
hybrid cultural norms that are not simply the sum of
their members' backgrounds. Scenarios for these teams
should reflect the team's actual culture, not an
abstraction of its members' national origins.

## Working with AI on Culturally Situated Scenarios

AI can help generate culturally situated scenarios—but
requires explicit, specific guidance to avoid defaulting
to the unmarked generic.

**Effective prompting approach:**

Provide the full cultural brief before asking for content:
- Specific country/region and industry
- Relevant cultural norms for this scenario's decision points
- Character backgrounds and cultural contexts
- Any regulatory or legal specifics that apply

Then ask AI to generate and explicitly flag any elements
it is uncertain about or has made assumptions on.

Treat AI-generated culturally situated content as a
first draft that requires cultural review—not a finished
product. Cultural specificity is one of the areas where
AI generation error is least visible from the outside
and most damaging to learner trust.

## Culturally Situated Scenario Writing: Patterns Worth Examining

- **Diversity markers without cultural texture**—diverse
  names and character appearances applied to a scenario
  that remains culturally unmarked and Anglo-American in
  its dynamics, norms, and assumptions
- **The universal workplace**—designing scenarios as if
  professional norms are the same everywhere and the
  "correct" response transcends cultural context
- **Cultural difference as problem**—framing non-Western
  or non-dominant cultural norms as the complicating factor
  that needs to be managed or corrected
- **Skipping the review**—publishing culturally situated
  scenarios without review from someone with genuine
  knowledge of that context
- **Regulatory transplanting**—applying compliance or
  legal guidance from one jurisdiction to scenarios set
  in another
- **Stereotype as shortcut**—using cultural stereotypes
  to signal context quickly, producing scenarios that
  are recognizable but reductive
