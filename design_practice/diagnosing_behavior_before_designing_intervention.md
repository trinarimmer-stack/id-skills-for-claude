# Diagnosing Behavior Before Designing an Intervention

## What This Skill Is About

Before you design anything, you need to understand why people are behaving the way they are. That sounds obvious. It rarely happens in practice.

Most training requests arrive pre-diagnosed. "People aren't following the process—we need a course." "Managers aren't having difficult conversations—we need a workshop." The assumption baked into the request is that the problem lives inside the person: they don't know something, or they don't care enough, or they have a bias that needs correcting.

But most behavior that looks like a knowledge or attitude problem is actually a rational response to the environment people are working in. When you design a training intervention for a systems problem, you waste resources, frustrate people, and—worst of all—implicitly blame individuals for something that isn't their fault.

This skill is about asking the right diagnostic questions before you pick up an authoring tool.

---

## Why It Matters

Thomas Gilbert, one of the founders of human performance technology, made an observation in the 1970s that still holds: most performance problems are caused by the environment, not the person. His Behavior Engineering Model identifies six factors that influence performance—and four of them are environmental (data, resources, incentives, consequences) while only two are individual (knowledge/skills and capacity).

That ratio—roughly 4:1 environment to person—is a useful gut check every time you receive a training request. Before assuming the problem is in someone's head, ask what in their environment might be making the current behavior the rational choice.

Julie Dirksen, in *Design for How People Learn*, frames it this way: there's a meaningful difference between a *knowing* gap, a *doing* gap, and an *environment* gap. Someone might know exactly what to do, be fully motivated to do it, and still not do it—because the environment makes the right behavior harder than the wrong one. A course won't fix that. The environment needs to change.

Mager and Pipe's classic diagnostic question from *Analyzing Performance Problems* is still one of the most useful tools in an ID's kit: *Could they do it if their life depended on it?* If yes—they have the knowledge and skill. The problem is somewhere else. Stop designing training and start asking different questions.

---

## The Four Behavior Drivers to Diagnose

Before selecting any intervention, examine all four:

**1. Knowledge and Skill**
Do people actually know what to do and how to do it? This is the only driver that training directly addresses. If the answer is no—they genuinely don't know—training is appropriate. If the answer is yes, keep digging.

**2. Motivation and Confidence**
Do people want to do it? Do they believe they can? Motivation problems are often misread as knowledge problems. Someone who lacks confidence in a skill may look like they don't understand it. Someone who doesn't see the point of a process may look like they weren't trained. The fix is different in each case—and training alone rarely solves a motivation problem.

**3. Environment and Resources**
Does the environment make the desired behavior easy or hard? Do people have the tools, time, access, and information they need? Are there physical or structural barriers? A new employee who doesn't follow the process may not have been given clear enough documentation. A manager who avoids difficult conversations may be operating in a culture where those conversations are implicitly punished.

**4. Incentives and Consequences**
What happens when people do the right thing? What happens when they don't? If the consequences of doing it wrong are low—or the consequences of doing it right are unpleasant—behavior will follow the incentives, not the training. Roger Chevalier's updated version of Gilbert's model calls this the most underdiagnosed driver in organizational performance. People aren't irrational. They respond to what's actually rewarded and penalized in their environment.

## The Diagnosis-First Discipline

The Bias Gap framework—developed by behavior-focused change practitioners—makes a pointed observation: when organizations label a performance problem as a "bias," they almost always end up targeting the person instead of the system. Awareness training gets designed. The behavior doesn't change. The system that was producing the behavior remains untouched.

The same failure mode appears across L&D more broadly. We reach for training because training is what we know how to build. But as Geary Rummler and Alan Brache argued in *Improving Performance*, most of the white space on an organizational chart—the gaps between roles, processes, and systems—is where performance actually breaks down. Training doesn't reach white space.

A useful reframe from systems thinking: before labeling behavior as a problem with a person, ask *what would make the current behavior the rational choice?* If you can answer that question clearly, you've found your real intervention point.

---

## The Diagnostic Questions Worth Asking

Use these before committing to any intervention:

**On knowledge and skill:**
- Could they do this correctly if their job depended on it?
- Have they ever done it correctly before?
- Is this a new skill or a degraded one?

**On motivation and confidence:**
- Do they know why this matters?
- Do they believe they're capable of doing it?
- Have they had negative experiences with this behavior before?

**On environment and resources:**
- Do they have everything they need to do it correctly?
- Is the process clear and accessible at the moment they need it?
- Are there physical, structural, or time-based barriers?

**On incentives and consequences:**
- What happens when they do it right?
- What happens when they do it wrong?
- Does the current reward structure accidentally reinforce the wrong behavior?

## Matching the Intervention to the Diagnosis

| Root Cause | Appropriate Intervention |
|---|---|
| Knowledge/skill gap | Training, job aids, practice, coaching |
| Motivation/confidence gap | Mentoring, relevance-building, success experiences, psychological safety work |
| Environment/resource gap | Process redesign, tool improvement, documentation, structural change |
| Incentive/consequence gap | Management practice change, recognition redesign, consequence restructuring |

Training is the right answer when—and only when—the diagnosis points to a genuine knowledge or skill gap. For everything else, training is at best a waste of time and at worst a signal to your people that the organization thinks the problem is them.

---

## What This Means for Instructional Designers

IDs are increasingly asked to function as performance consultants—not just course builders. That shift requires a diagnostic discipline that most ID training doesn't emphasize enough.

It means being willing to come back to a stakeholder and say: *"I've looked at this, and I don't think a course is the right solution. Here's what I think is actually going on, and here's what I'd recommend instead."*

That conversation requires courage. It also requires a clear diagnostic framework so you can defend the recommendation with evidence rather than intuition.

The goal is never to avoid building training. It's to ensure that when you build it, it's addressing something training can actually fix.

## Patterns Worth Examining

**Reaching for training because it's familiar**
Training is what L&D knows how to produce. When the only tool you have is a course, every problem looks like a knowledge gap. The diagnostic step gets skipped because the solution feels obvious before the analysis begins.

**Mistaking compliance for behavior change**
Someone completing a course proves they completed a course. It doesn't prove they changed what they do on the job. When training is evaluated by completion rates rather than behavior outcomes, it creates the illusion of progress while the underlying problem persists.

**Labeling system problems as individual failures**
When people don't do what the organization wants, the instinct is to explain it as a personal deficit—a bias, a skill gap, an attitude problem. This locates the problem in the person and designs the intervention accordingly. It leaves the system that produced the behavior completely intact.

**Designing for awareness when the problem is environment**
Awareness training—courses about unconscious bias, safety culture, communication norms—is often used as a first response to systemic performance problems. It signals organizational concern without requiring structural change. Awareness rarely moves behavior when the environment is actively working against it.

**Skipping the "would they if they could?" question**
Mager and Pipe's diagnostic question is a 30-second intervention that can save weeks of wasted development. It almost never gets asked before the project kickoff meeting.
