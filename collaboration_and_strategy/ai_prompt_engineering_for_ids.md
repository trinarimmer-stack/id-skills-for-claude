# Instructional Design Skill: AI Prompt Engineering 
for Instructional Designers

## Overview
Prompt engineering is the practice of crafting inputs 
to AI systems that produce useful, high-quality outputs. 
For instructional designers, it's becoming a core 
professional skill — the difference between AI that 
generates generic, unusable content and AI that functions 
as a genuine design partner. This skill helps Claude 
support instructional designers in writing effective 
prompts for common ID tasks, evaluating AI outputs 
with a critical instructional eye, and building 
reusable prompt frameworks for their practice.

---

## Core Principle: AI Is Only as Good as the Brief You Give It
Prompting AI is not unlike briefing a talented but 
context-free collaborator. The more clearly you 
communicate the task, the audience, the constraints, 
and the success criteria, the more useful the output.

Vague prompt → generic output → heavy revision required
Specific prompt → targeted output → light revision required

The goal is not to get AI to do your thinking for you. 
It's to offload the right tasks so you can focus 
your expertise where it matters most.

## The Anatomy of an Effective Prompt

A strong prompt typically includes some combination of:

### 1. Role
Tell the AI what perspective or expertise to bring.

> *"You are an experienced instructional designer 
> specializing in compliance training for 
> financial services..."*

> *"Act as a skeptical subject matter expert 
> reviewing this learning objective for accuracy..."*

### 2. Context
Provide the background information the AI needs 
to give relevant output.

> *"This course is for newly hired customer service 
> representatives with no prior financial industry 
> experience. The organization uses a formal 
> communication style. Training is delivered 
> via Articulate Storyline on desktop only."*

### 3. Task
Be specific about what you want the AI to produce.

> *"Write three scenario-based multiple choice 
> questions that assess the learner's ability 
> to identify phishing attempts in work email."*

Not: *"Write some quiz questions about phishing."*

### 4. Format
Specify how you want the output structured.

> *"Format each question with: the scenario, 
> four answer options labeled A–D, the correct 
> answer, and a 2–3 sentence explanation of 
> why it's correct."*

### 5. Constraints
Define what to avoid or what limits apply.

> *"Keep each question under 100 words. 
> Avoid technical jargon. Do not use 
> the words 'phishing' or 'cybersecurity' 
> in the question stem."*

### 6. Success Criteria
Tell the AI what good looks like.

> *"Each question should require the learner 
> to apply judgment, not just recall a definition. 
> Wrong answers should reflect common real-world 
> mistakes, not obvious errors."*

## Prompt Frameworks for Common ID Tasks

### Needs Analysis Support
You are an experienced performance consultant. 
I'm meeting with a stakeholder who has requested 
[type of training] for [audience] in [industry/context]. 

Generate 10 diagnostic questions I should ask 
to determine whether training is actually the 
right solution and, if so, what kind.

Focus on uncovering: the nature of the performance gap, 
whether it's a can't-do or won't-do problem, 
what's been tried before, and what success 
would look like on the job.
### Learning Objective Writing
I am designing a course on [topic] for [audience]. 
The business goal is [goal].

Write 5 learning objectives that:
Use observable, measurable action verbs
Target application or higher (Bloom's Taxonomy)
Describe job performance, not content coverage
Are specific enough to design an assessment from

Avoid verbs like "understand," "know," or "be aware of."
### Scenario Development
You are an instructional designer writing a 
branching scenario for [audience] on [topic].

Context: [brief description of job role and situation]
Performance objective: [the objective this scenario supports]
Common mistake to address: [the misconception or error 
learners typically make]

Write a scenario with:
A believable character and realistic situation
One meaningful decision point with 3 options:
  one best choice, one common mistake, 
  one avoidant/passive choice
Consequence feedback for each option that shows 
  realistic outcomes and explains the reasoning
Conversational dialogue that sounds like real people, 
  not a textbook

Tone: [formal/conversational/direct]
Length: [approximate word count per section]
### Content Simplification
I have the following source material written 
by a subject matter expert. My audience is 
[audience description] with [experience level] 
experience in this area.

Rewrite this content for e-learning delivery:
Target a [grade level] reading level
Use second person, active voice
Break into chunks of no more than [word count] per screen
Lead with what learners need to DO, not what they need to know
Cut any content that isn't essential for job performance
Replace jargon with plain language where possible

[Paste source content here]
### Feedback Writing
I have the following assessment question and answer options.

[Paste question and options]

Write feedback for each option that:
For the correct answer: confirms the choice and 
  reinforces the reasoning in 2–3 sentences
For each incorrect answer: acknowledges the choice 
  without shaming, explains the real-world consequence, 
  and redirects to correct thinking
Does not use the words "correct," "incorrect," 
  "right," or "wrong"
Sounds like a knowledgeable, supportive colleague
Is no more than 3 sentences per option
### SME Interview Preparation
I'm preparing to interview a subject matter expert 
about [topic] for a course targeting [audience].

Generate:
1. 5 opening questions to understand the scope 
   and business context
2. 5 questions to uncover what good and poor 
   performance looks like on the job
3. 5 questions to identify the most common 
   mistakes or misconceptions
4. 3 questions to prioritize content 
   (need to know vs. nice to know)
5. 2 closing questions to identify resources 
   and flag anything I might have missed

I want to understand performance, not just content.
### Storyboard Review
Review the following e-learning storyboard excerpt 
as an experienced instructional designer.

Evaluate it against these criteria:
Alignment to the stated learning objective
Cognitive load (is there too much on one screen?)
Voice and tone (second person, active voice, 
  appropriate reading level)
Interaction quality (does it require real thinking?)
Feedback quality (does it explain the why?)
Accessibility considerations

Flag specific issues and suggest concrete revisions.
Do not rewrite the entire storyboard — 
identify the 3 most important improvements.

[Paste storyboard content]
### Course Outline Generation
I am designing a [length] course on [topic] 
for [audience] with [experience level].

The business goal is: [goal]
The top 3 things learners must be able to DO 
after this course: [list]

Generate a course outline that:
Sequences content from foundational to complex
Names modules based on what learners will be 
  able to do (not what will be covered)
Includes 3–5 lessons per module
Identifies the primary interaction type 
  for each lesson (scenario, simulation, 
  knowledge check, reflection, etc.)
Flags any prerequisite knowledge 
  learners will need

Keep total seat time to approximately [X] minutes.

## Evaluating AI Output: The ID Quality Checklist

AI output always requires a human evaluation pass. 
Apply these checks before using any AI-generated 
content in a course:

### Instructional Quality
- [ ] Does this serve the learning objective?
- [ ] Does it require application, not just recall?
- [ ] Is the cognitive demand appropriate 
      for the audience?
- [ ] Does feedback explain the *why*?

### Accuracy
- [ ] Is the content factually correct?
- [ ] Has a subject matter expert reviewed it?
- [ ] Are there any confident-sounding errors 
      (AI hallucinations)?
- [ ] Are statistics, citations, and references verified?

### Audience Fit
- [ ] Is the reading level appropriate?
- [ ] Does the tone match the organizational culture?
- [ ] Are examples relevant to this specific audience?
- [ ] Is language inclusive and accessible?

### Writing Quality
- [ ] Is it in second person, active voice?
- [ ] Is it as concise as it can be?
- [ ] Does it sound like a human wrote it?
- [ ] Has generic filler been cut?

### Bias and Sensitivity
- [ ] Are scenarios and characters diverse 
      and representative?
- [ ] Is any content culturally insensitive 
      or exclusionary?
- [ ] Does it reflect realistic job complexity 
      rather than idealized situations?

## Advanced Prompting Techniques

### Chain of Thought Prompting
Ask the AI to show its reasoning before 
producing the output. Useful for complex 
instructional decisions.

> *"Before writing the learning objectives, 
> walk me through your reasoning: what level 
> of Bloom's Taxonomy is appropriate here 
> and why? Then write the objectives."*

### Iterative Refinement
Treat the first output as a draft, not a final product. 
Follow up with specific revision instructions.

> *"The scenario is good but the dialogue feels 
> too formal for frontline retail staff. 
> Rewrite the dialogue to sound more natural — 
> shorter sentences, contractions, 
> the way people actually talk at work."*

### Persona Prompting
Ask AI to respond from a specific perspective 
to stress-test your design.

> *"Review this course outline as a skeptical 
> senior manager who thinks training is 
> usually a waste of time. What questions 
> would you ask? What would make you 
> doubt its value?"*

> *"Read this scenario as a learner who has 
> been doing this job for 10 years and 
> thinks they already know everything. 
> What would make them disengage?"*

### Constraint-Based Prompting
Adding constraints often improves output quality 
by forcing focus.

> *"Write this in under 75 words."*
> *"Use no more than three bullet points."*
> *"Do not mention any specific software tools."*
> *"Write for someone who has never heard 
> of instructional design."*

### Few-Shot Prompting
Provide examples of what good output looks like 
before asking for the actual output.

> *"Here are two examples of the feedback style 
> I'm looking for: [example 1] [example 2]. 
> Using this same style, write feedback for 
> the following question and answer options..."*

## Building a Personal Prompt Library

The most efficient prompt engineers don't 
start from scratch every time. They build 
a library of tested, reusable prompt templates 
for their most common tasks.

### What to Save
- Prompts that produced excellent outputs 
  on the first or second try
- Prompts for tasks you do repeatedly 
  (objective writing, feedback, scenario drafts)
- Prompts that saved significant revision time

### How to Organize
Organize by task type:
- Needs Analysis
- Objective Writing
- Content Development
- Scenario & Branching
- Assessment & Feedback
- Editing & Review
- Stakeholder Communication

### How to Maintain
- Note what worked and what needed refinement
- Update templates when you find a better approach
- Tag templates by audience type or industry 
  for faster retrieval

## Ethical Considerations for AI in ID Practice

### Transparency
Be clear with stakeholders and clients about 
when and how AI is being used in your work. 
Establish shared expectations early.

### Accuracy Responsibility
AI can be wrong with confidence. The designer 
is responsible for the accuracy of every 
piece of content that goes into a course — 
regardless of whether AI generated it.

### Learner Data Privacy
Be thoughtful about what learner data is 
shared with AI tools. Review your organization's 
data governance policies before using AI 
on sensitive or confidential projects.

### Intellectual Property
Understand the IP implications of your 
AI tools — what data they're trained on, 
who owns the outputs, and whether outputs 
may inadvertently reproduce copyrighted content.

### Quality Over Speed
AI makes it possible to produce more content 
faster. That's only valuable if the content 
is instructionally sound. The temptation to 
ship faster should never override the 
responsibility to ship better.

## Prompt Engineering: Patterns Worth Examining

- **Too vague** — *"Write a course about safety"* 
  produces unusable output
- **No audience context** — generic prompts 
  produce generic content
- **Accepting first output** — the first draft 
  is rarely the best; iterate
- **No format specification** — AI will choose 
  a format; it may not be the one you need
- **Skipping the quality check** — AI errors 
  are confident and easy to miss on a quick read
- **Over-relying on AI for judgment calls** — 
  what content to include, what interactions 
  to design, what tone to use — these require 
  human expertise
- **No prompt library** — reinventing the wheel 
  for every project wastes the efficiency gains 
  AI provides
