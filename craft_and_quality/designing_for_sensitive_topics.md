# Instructional Design Skill: Designing for Sensitive Topics

## Overview
Some of the most important learning experiences an instructional
designer will ever create involve content that is emotionally
charged, culturally complex, personally risky, or professionally
uncomfortable. DEI training, mental health and wellbeing content,
trauma-informed design, workplace harassment, grief, addiction,
ethical dilemmas, and crisis response all require a calibration
that goes beyond instructional technique.

This skill helps Claude support instructional designers in handling
sensitive content with appropriate weight, tonal precision, and
genuine care—and in knowing when and how to use levity as a
counterweight to difficult material.

---

## Core Principle: The Designer Holds the Material

Sensitive content requires a designer who can feel the weight of
the material and make deliberate choices about how to carry it.

LLMs default to one of two failure modes with sensitive topics:
clinical detachment (handling the content at arm's length, as if
it were any other compliance topic) or performative empathy
(language that signals awareness of the topic's gravity without
actually engaging with it). Neither serves learners.

A skilled designer *holds* difficult content—acknowledges its
weight, treats it with specificity, and makes choices that respect
the real human experiences the training is about.

---

## Recognizing Sensitive Content

Sensitive content includes but is not limited to:

- **Identity and systemic inequity:** DEI training, anti-racism,
  gender equity, disability inclusion, LGBTQ+ workplace inclusion
- **Interpersonal harm:** Harassment, discrimination, bullying,
  retaliation, coercion
- **Mental health and wellbeing:** Stress, burnout, anxiety,
  depression, suicide prevention, trauma response
- **Substance use and addiction:** Workplace substance policies,
  employee assistance, recovery
- **Grief and loss:** Bereavement, organizational loss, critical
  incidents
- **Ethical pressure:** Whistleblowing, conflicts of interest,
  pressure to act against values
- **Crisis and safety:** Emergency response, active threat
  situations, medical emergencies
- **Culturally loaded topics:** Content that carries different
  weight or meaning across cultural contexts

## Tonal Calibration: The Core Design Skill

### What "Getting the Tone Right" Actually Means

Tone in sensitive content is not about being careful or cautious.
It is about being precise. The right tone:

- Acknowledges the reality of the experience without dramatizing it
- Treats learners as capable adults who can handle difficult
  material when it is presented with respect
- Does not minimize, euphemize, or sanitize in ways that signal
  the topic is too uncomfortable to address directly
- Does not catastrophize or use emotional manipulation to drive
  engagement
- Centers the people most affected by the issue, not the
  organization's discomfort with it

### The Empathy-Utility Balance

Sensitive training often sits in tension between emotional
acknowledgment and practical skill-building. Both are necessary.

- Too much emotional acknowledgment without practical guidance
  leaves learners with heightened awareness and no tools
- Too much practical guidance without emotional acknowledgment
  signals that the human dimension of the issue doesn't matter

The designer's job is to find the sequence and proportion that
serves the specific learner population. For trauma-adjacent
content, acknowledgment typically comes first. For skill-building
content (how to respond when you witness harassment), utility
may be the primary purpose with empathy embedded in the scenario
design rather than stated explicitly.

### Avoiding the Performative Register

Performative empathy is language that performs awareness of
sensitivity without actually engaging with it. Common markers:

- Lengthy disclaimers before the content begins
- Sentences that name the difficulty without sitting with it
  ("We know these conversations can be hard...")
- Centering the organization's values rather than the learner's
  experience ("At [Company], we believe...")
- Using passive constructions that avoid naming who does harm
  ("When incidents occur..." rather than "When someone harasses
  a colleague...")

Replace performative language with specific, direct, human language
that treats the topic as real and the learner as capable.

## Specific Design Guidance by Content Type

### DEI and Anti-Racism Content

- Name systems, not just behaviors. Individual bias training
  that ignores structural inequity misrepresents the problem.
- Center the experiences of people most affected by inequity—
  do not center the discomfort of people learning about it.
- Avoid the "diversity as business case" framing as the primary
  argument. It instrumentalizes people's identities.
- Scenarios should reflect genuine ambiguity and complexity—
  not obvious villains and obvious heroes.
- Have content reviewed by people with lived experience of the
  inequities being addressed. This is not optional.

### Harassment and Misconduct Content

- Use specific, realistic language about what harassment looks
  and sounds like. Vague descriptions ("inappropriate behavior")
  allow learners to exclude themselves from the scenario.
- Do not design scenarios where the "correct" response is obvious
  from the setup. Real harassment situations are often ambiguous,
  power-imbalanced, and socially risky to respond to.
- Acknowledge the organizational dynamics that make reporting
  difficult—retaliation risk, disbelief, career consequences.
  Training that ignores these dynamics is not preparing learners
  for reality.
- Bystander intervention training requires practicing the specific
  uncomfortable moment of choosing to act—not just knowing that
  acting is correct.

### Mental Health Content

- Use plain, destigmatizing language. "Mental health" not
  "mental illness" as the default frame. Normalize help-seeking
  throughout, not just in a resources slide at the end.
- If the content involves suicide or self-harm, follow safe
  messaging guidelines (developed by mental health organizations)
  and have the content reviewed by a mental health professional.
- Do not conflate stress (a normal human experience) with
  clinical conditions. This both minimizes serious conditions
  and pathologizes normal responses to difficult work.
- Psychological safety in the training itself matters—learners
  should feel the training environment is safe before engaging
  with vulnerable material.

### Ethical Pressure Scenarios

- The most effective ethics scenarios place learners in situations
  where the right thing to do is also the socially or
  professionally costly thing to do. If the correct choice
  has no downside, it's not an ethics scenario.
- Acknowledge that organizational culture, hierarchy, and
  power dynamics are real constraints on ethical action—
  and design for navigating those constraints, not just
  knowing the right answer.

## Levity as a Design Tool

### When Levity Belongs

Humor and lightness are not the opposite of seriousness—they are
tools for managing emotional load, building rapport, and making
difficult material approachable. Used well, levity:

- Releases tension at moments when learners need to breathe
- Makes a dry or uncomfortable topic feel more human
- Builds trust between the learning experience and the learner
- Signals that the designer is a real person, not an
  institutional voice

### When Levity Does Not Belong

Levity is inappropriate when:

- It minimizes or trivializes the experiences of people who
  have been harmed
- It provides emotional cover for the learner to avoid genuine
  engagement with difficult material
- It is used to make the *organization* more comfortable rather
  than to serve the learner
- The topic involves active trauma, grief, or crisis

A rule of thumb: levity can exist in the *frame* around difficult
content (the setup, the facilitator voice, the pacing between
sections) but rarely in the content itself when the subject
involves real harm to real people.

### Executing Levity Well

LLMs produce humor that feels forced, generic, or tonally
mismatched. Effective levity in e-learning:

- Is specific to the situation, not generic wordplay
- Comes from character and voice, not from jokes
- Lands in the setup or framing, not in the punchline
- Punches sideways or up—never at the expense of people
  with less power
- Earns the moment—levity that arrives before trust is
  built falls flat or offends

The best test: would a thoughtful, caring colleague use this
tone in a real conversation about this topic? If not, reconsider.

## Process Guidance for Sensitive Content Projects

### Before You Design

- Identify who is most affected by the issue the training addresses
- Determine who needs to review the content for lived experience,
  cultural accuracy, and professional expertise
- Clarify the organization's actual commitment to the topic—
  training that isn't backed by policy, accountability, or
  leadership modeling will not produce behavior change and
  may do harm by signaling performative rather than genuine commitment
- Establish psychological safety guidelines for any facilitated
  components

### During Design

- Have a subject matter expert and a lived-experience reviewer
  in the loop from storyboard stage—not just at final review
- Review language specifically for euphemism, passive voice,
  and centering the wrong perspective
- Test scenarios for whether the "correct" response requires
  genuine moral courage or just reading comprehension
- Check that resources and support information is specific,
  current, and accessible—not a generic EAP slide

### At Review

- Include at least one reviewer who represents the population
  most affected by the content
- Review explicitly for tone—not just accuracy
- Ask: does this training prepare learners for the actual
  complexity of these situations, or does it prepare them
  for a simpler version?

## Designing for Sensitive Topics: Patterns Worth Examining

- **The awareness-only course**—training that raises awareness
  without building skill. Awareness without tools can increase
  anxiety without improving outcomes.
- **Centering the uncomfortable majority**—DEI or harassment
  training designed primarily to make the dominant group
  comfortable rather than to protect or empower those most at risk.
- **The obvious scenario**—situations where the right answer
  is clear from the setup. Real sensitive situations are
  almost never this clear.
- **Resources as absolution**—a slide of EAP numbers at the
  end of a mental health course does not constitute genuine
  support design.
- **Humor that covers**—using levity to let learners (or the
  organization) off the hook for genuine engagement with
  difficult material.
- **One-and-done**—treating a single training event as
  sufficient for deep behavior or culture change. Sensitive
  topics require sustained, reinforced learning ecosystems,
  not annual compliance completions.
