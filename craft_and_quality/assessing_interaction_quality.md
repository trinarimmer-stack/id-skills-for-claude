# Instructional Design Skill: Assessing Interaction Quality

## Overview
Knowing how to *design* a scenario, branching interaction, or
decision-making activity is one skill. Knowing how to *evaluate*
one—and articulate specifically why it works or doesn't—is another.
This skill file supports instructional designers (and those coaching
them) in assessing the quality of a single interaction: a branching
scenario, a decision-making practice, a scenario-based question, or
a case study.

Use this file when:
- Reviewing a peer's or learner's work as an ID coach or mentor
- Self-assessing your own interaction designs before submission
- Giving structured, actionable feedback on scenario quality
- Helping a less experienced ID understand what "great" looks like
  versus "good" or "needs work"

---

## Core Principle: The Professional Wince Test

Before applying any rubric, ask one question:

**Would a working professional in this role wince at this scenario?**

Show the interaction to someone who does this job every day.
Would they recognize the situation as authentic to their
experience—or would they immediately see it as artificial,
constructed, or oversimplified?

Great interactions pass this test. Poor ones don't survive it.
Everything in the rubric below is in service of this single standard.

## The Six-Dimension Rubric

---

### Dimension 1: Scenario Realism
*Does this feel like a real situation, or a training situation?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- Specific, lived-in details: a recognizable context, real-sounding
  stakes, a situation the target learner would immediately place
  themselves in
- Ambiguity and competing priorities are present—just like the actual job
- The learner recognizes themselves (or a colleague) in the scenario
  before they even reach the decision point

**Signals of good quality:**
- Plausible but generic—could happen anywhere, to anyone
- Competent and functional, but unlikely to generate strong
  identification or engagement

**Signals of poor quality:**
- Obviously constructed to illustrate a lesson
- The learner immediately recognizes they're in "the training
  scenario"—stakes feel artificial, characters feel like props
- Situation is sanitized: no ambiguity, no competing priorities, no mess

**Coaching prompt:**
*"Would someone who does this job every day recognize this situation?
Or would they recognize it as a training scenario?"*

---

### Dimension 2: Choice Architecture
*Are the options meaningfully different, or is one answer obviously right?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- Every option is defensible—a reasonable, well-intentioned person
  could choose any of them under pressure or with incomplete information
- Wrong choices reflect real cognitive traps, common rationalizations,
  or competing priorities—not laziness or ignorance
- All choices are similar in length and surface plausibility
- The correct answer does not always appear in the same position
- Each distractor reflects a coherent but flawed mental model, not
  just a weaker version of the right answer

**Signals of good quality:**
- Correct answer is clear to someone who knows the material
- Distractors are plausible enough to require thought, but somewhat
  easier to eliminate than they should be

**Signals of poor quality:**
- One answer is obviously right on the surface
- Distractors are too short, too extreme, or clearly absurd
- Answer-position bias: the correct answer is almost always A or
  consistently in the same position
- A learner could pass without exercising real judgment—general
  intelligence or test-taking instinct is enough

**Note for AI-assisted design:**
AI tends to generate one thorough, professional-sounding option and
two shorter, more reactive, or more extreme choices. This is one of
the most common failure modes in AI-generated interactions. When
using AI to draft choices, instruct it explicitly: make all options
similar in length, ensure each distractor reflects a coherent but
flawed mental model, and vary the position of the correct answer
across decision nodes.

**Coaching prompt:**
*"Could a thoughtful, experienced person reasonably choose any of
these options? Or is one clearly 'the training answer'?"*

---

### Dimension 3: Consequence Design
*Does the learner feel the outcome of their choice, or are they
simply told whether they were right or wrong?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- Consequences are shown, not told—the learner experiences what
  happens as a result of their choice before any explanation arrives
- Wrong choices lead to realistic downstream problems: a difficult
  conversation, a missed deadline, a confused stakeholder, a
  compliance flag
- Feedback is an outcome first, an explanation second
- The learner feels the weight of the decision—even (especially)
  when they get it right

**Signals of good quality:**
- Feedback explains why the choice was right or wrong with genuine
  contextual reasoning
- Some consequence language is present, even if stated rather than
  dramatized

**Signals of poor quality:**
- "Correct!" or "Incorrect, try again"—verdict without consequence
- The learner learns nothing from failure except that they failed
- Feedback is identical regardless of which wrong choice was made
- Correct-answer feedback congratulates the learner on technique
  rather than letting them feel the outcome

**Coaching prompt:**
*"If a learner chooses the wrong option, what happens to them?
Do they feel it—or are they just told about it?"*

---

### Dimension 4: Cognitive Load Calibration
*Does the scenario give learners exactly what they need to
decide—nothing more, nothing less?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- Precise: every detail in the scenario earns its place
- The learner has enough context to make a meaningful decision
  without being overwhelmed by setup
- Complexity comes from the decision itself, not from navigating
  the scenario structure
- Extraneous detail has been removed; missing context has been supplied

**Signals of good quality:**
- Slightly too much or too little context, but workable
- The learner can still make a meaningful choice; the scenario
  doesn't collapse under its own weight

**Signals of poor quality:**
- Information dump: so much setup that the learner is fatigued
  before they reach the decision
- So sparse that the choice feels arbitrary or underdetermined
- The scenario does too much work—or not enough—to support the
  decision it's asking for

**Coaching prompt:**
*"Does every piece of information in this scenario serve the decision
the learner needs to make? What could be cut? What's missing?"*

---

### Dimension 5: Objective Alignment
*Is the learner actually practicing the skill, or just answering
a question about it?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- The choice the learner makes *is* the skill being practiced—not
  a question *about* the skill
- The interaction couldn't be about anything else; it's purpose-built
  for this objective
- A learner cannot pass on general intelligence or test-taking
  instinct alone; they must apply the target skill

**Signals of good quality:**
- Related to the objective but slightly indirect
- A learner could get it right without fully demonstrating the
  target skill, but the interaction still builds relevant thinking

**Signals of poor quality:**
- A knowledge check dressed up as a scenario: surface-level context
  wrapped around what is essentially a recall question
- The scenario could be stripped of its context and still function
  as a straightforward multiple-choice question
- Learner is asked to identify what someone *should* do rather than
  actually practice doing it

**Coaching prompt:**
*"Is this practicing the skill—or practicing knowing about the skill?
Could you answer this correctly without ever having done the job?"*

---

### Dimension 6: Feedback Depth and Specificity
*Does feedback treat the learner as a professional who made a
reasonable but incorrect judgment?*

**Signals of great quality:**
- Feedback addresses the specific reasoning behind each wrong
  choice—not just why the right answer is right
- Each distractor gets its own tailored feedback that respects
  the logic of that choice and explains precisely where it breaks down
- Feedback speaks to the learner as a peer, not a student: "Here's
  why that instinct makes sense—and here's where it leads you wrong"
- Right-answer feedback reinforces the reasoning, not just the result

**Signals of good quality:**
- Feedback clearly explains the correct answer and why it matters
- Some differentiation between wrong-answer feedback, even if not
  fully tailored to each distractor

**Signals of poor quality:**
- Generic wrong-answer feedback ("That's not quite right. The correct
  answer is...")
- Feedback is identical regardless of which wrong choice was made
- Feedback lectures: dumps information instead of addressing the
  specific error in judgment
- Correct-answer feedback congratulates the learner rather than
  reinforcing the learning

**Coaching prompt:**
*"If I chose Option B and got it wrong, does the feedback explain why
Option B was wrong—or does it just tell me Option A was right?"*

## Reading the Whole Picture

No single dimension defines quality. A strong interaction can have a
slightly generic scenario and still be excellent if the choice
architecture and consequence design are exceptional. Assess the
interaction as a whole, not as a checklist.

**The most common failure pattern in newer IDs:**
They nail the content and miss the consequence. They know what the
right answer is and they teach it clearly. What they haven't yet
internalized is that learning happens in the moment of *feeling the
outcome*—not in the moment of being told they were wrong. This is
the gap between good and great, and it's the most important thing
to develop in an ID who is ready to level up.

**The second most common failure pattern:**
Plausible scenarios with implausible choices. The situation feels
real; the options feel like a quiz. When choices don't reflect the
genuine range of decisions people actually make under pressure, the
scenario loses its power regardless of how well-written the setup is.

## Using This Rubric in Coaching Conversations

When reviewing an interaction with a newer ID, resist the impulse
to rewrite it for them. Instead:

1. **Name what's working first.** Specifically. Not "this is good"
   but "the character setup here is strong—Jordan feels like a real
   person, not a training prop."

2. **Ask before telling.** "What were you going for with the second
   choice? Walk me through your thinking." Their answer will tell you
   whether this is a craft problem or a concept problem—and those
   require different coaching.

3. **Focus on one dimension at a time.** Trying to address all six
   dimensions in one conversation is overwhelming. Identify the
   highest-leverage gap and work there.

4. **Use the coaching prompts.** The questions embedded in each
   dimension are designed to help a designer see the gap themselves,
   rather than being told about it. Self-discovery sticks; feedback
   doesn't always.

5. **Return to the Professional Wince Test.** When in doubt, come
   back to the single question that anchors all six dimensions:
   *Would someone who does this job every day recognize this as real?*

## Patterns Worth Examining

- **The "gotcha" scenario** — deliberately obscure setups or trick
  questions that frustrate rather than develop judgment; often
  confused with "rigor"
- **The telegraphed answer** — one option is so clearly right that
  the interaction becomes a formality; learners feel talked down to
- **The consequence-free wrong answer** — learner clicks the wrong
  option and is told so, but nothing happens; no downstream effect,
  no felt consequence, no learning
- **The information dump setup** — so much context in the scenario
  stem that learners are fatigued before they reach the decision
- **Feedback that lectures** — wrong-answer feedback that dumps
  content instead of addressing the specific error in judgment
- **The knowledge check in disguise** — scenario framing wrapped
  around what is functionally a recall question; looks like applied
  practice but isn't
- **Generic characters** — learners disengage from characters they
  can't place or relate to; specificity builds investment
- **Naming the mechanism, not the experience** — labeling an
  interaction "Branching Scenario" or "Decision Point" at the
  learner-facing level breaks immersion; titles should name the
  experience ("The Escalation Call"), not the template type
